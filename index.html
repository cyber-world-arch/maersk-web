NotPetya & Maersk:
The $10 Billion Cyberweapon

The most destructive cyberattack in history — a digital weapon disguised as ransomware that caused over $10 billion in global damage.

Investigation Report

On June 27, 2017, Maersk’s global operations collapsed within minutes. Across offices, ports, and shipping terminals, screens flickered before displaying a chilling message: “Repairing file system on C:”.

Within moments, the true payload revealed itself. A destructive ransomware variant had begun wiping corporate networks across continents.

76 port terminals. 48,000 laptops. 4,000 servers. One company brought to a standstill in under seven minutes.

This was NotPetya — a cyberweapon masquerading as ransomware. Its purpose was not financial extortion, but irreversible destruction.

“This was not a crime. This was an act of cyberwarfare.”

The malware entered Maersk’s network through a compromised Ukrainian accounting software update. Once inside, it spread laterally using stolen NSA exploits including EternalBlue.

Domain controllers were wiped. Active Directory collapsed. Global authentication failed. Ports were unable to process cargo. Ships sat idle.

June 27, 2017 — 11:30 AM UTC

First infections detected in Ukraine.

11:37 AM UTC

Maersk headquarters in Copenhagen goes offline.

11:45 AM UTC

Global port terminals fail simultaneously.

12:00 PM UTC

Shipping operations worldwide halted.

The company lost its booking systems, cargo tracking, billing platforms, and internal communications in less than an hour.

Recovery was only possible due to a single domain controller in Ghana that was offline during the attack because of a power outage.

That one machine became the digital seed from which Maersk rebuilt its entire global infrastructure.

Total financial impact: Over $10 billion in global economic damage.

NotPetya reshaped how governments define cyberwarfare. It marked the first publicly acknowledged instance of a nation-state deploying a digital weapon against civilian infrastructure.
